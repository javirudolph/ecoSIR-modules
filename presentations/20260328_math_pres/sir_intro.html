Computational Literacy in Disease Ecology

Anjali Tomerlin

2026-03-28

What we built

An open, browser-based disease ecology module — no installation, no prior background required.

  • We built this module with computational literacy in mind.

Module Covers:

  • Epidemiological vocabulary and compartmental models
  • The SIR epidemic model and its biological assumptions
  • Solving ODE systems numerically and statistical approaches for estimation (MLE)

Currently in beta. Available at github.com/javirudolph/ecoSIR-modules. Coming to QUBES Hub.

Why we built this module

The SIR model is one of the most studied objects in all of applied mathematics. The gap is not content. The gap is the on-ramp.

Most existing resources assume the reader already thinks like a modeler:

  • They begin with equations and ask students to follow.
  • They treat computation as implementation of mathematics that is already understood.

This module starts from the other direction:

  • What biological process does this compartment represent?
  • What assumption produces this term in the equation?
  • What happens to the system if that assumption is violated?

Who is this module for?

Everyone!

  • Even students with no prior coding or disease ecology background (most students in most classrooms) can pick up this module and learn about SIR models in a way that is accessible and interactive.
  • This module is intended to be both a self-learning resource as well as a resource educators can use in their lessons.

What is computational literacy?

diSessa (2000) argues that computation is not merely a tool for more efficient instruction, but the basis for a new literacy that changes how people think and learn.


Like written language or mathematics, computational literacy is a representational medium — a way of constructing, expressing, and communicating ideas.

In disease ecology: you cannot fully understand an SIR model by reading its equations.

  • You have to build it, run it, break it, and interpret it to understand what it means.

Why do we care about computational literacy?

In the last 30 years, nearly every scientific field has developed a computational counterpart — bioinformatics, computational statistics, computational ecology.


The question is no longer whether students will use computation. It is whether they will understand what they are doing when they do.

What computational literacy is NOT

Not software training

  • Knowing how to use R, Python, or Excel is a starting point, not an endpoint. Tool fluency without conceptual grounding produces students who can execute code they do not understand.

Not generic computational thinking

  • Decomposition, abstraction, and algorithmic thinking are general skills but detached from any discipline. These are valuable foundations, but they are not sufficient for scientific practice.

What computational literacy is NOT

Not AI literacy

  • AI literacy concerns evaluating, using, and understanding AI systems. It overlaps with computational literacy but is distinct. (Hackl, Müller & Sailer, 2026)

Not mathematical fluency

  • A student can solve the SIR system analytically but have no idea what \(\beta\) means biologically, or whether their code faithfully represents it.

Computational literacy in a discipline means moving fluently between biological meaning, mathematical formulation, and computational implementation.

Why did we focus on computational literacy in this module?

Our goal is to create a module that focuses on making complex mathematical ideas accessible.


Key ideas from computational literacy and pedagogy helped us accomplish this:

  • Instructional Scaffolding
  • Bidirectional Translation
  • Immersive Learning

Instructional Scaffolding

What is it?

Not overwhelming the learner with everything at once but giving them the opportunity to access more information at their own pace. (Northern Illinois University Center for Innovative Teaching and Learning, 2012)


How did we implement this in our module?

Collapsible explanations

 

Why did we include this?

  • Appeal to audiences of all experience levels.

  • Allows teachers / students to choose how “in depth” they go.

Bidirectional Translation

What is it?

Although it usually applies to language learning, in our module it refers to the translation of equations and ideas into code.

 

How did we implement this in our module?

Sections first introduce a concept in words, then explain the mathematics behind it, and link those explanations and math to code.

 

Why did we include this?

  • Builds skills in translating ideas with real-world interpretations to code.
  • Important skill in the world of “big data.”

Immersive Learning

What is it?

Presentation of information in a way that is interactive and engaging. (Center for Engaged Learning, n.d.)

 

How did we implement this in our module?

Embedded Shiny apps and interactive code chunks

 

Why did we include this?

  • Appeal to learners that always ask the “what if” questions.
  • Applies Piaget’s constructivism: People learn better when they actively manipulate the material. (Ackermann, 2001)

The future of this project and takeaways

This is the first module in a series — a foundation for SEIR models, spillover dynamics, parameter estimation, and spatial spread.


While building this module, I had to “step out” of the math world. I learned valuable skills in both how and why we should make mathematical information accessible.


This isn’t only for math, though! Think about how you can apply these ideas to make your research more accessible to others.

 

Research is done in isolation, but change happens collectively.
