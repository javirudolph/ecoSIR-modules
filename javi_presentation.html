Computational literacy as a new medium

diSessa (2000) argues that computation is not merely a tool for more efficient instruction, but the basis for a new literacy that changes how people think and learn.

Like written language or mathematics, computational literacy is a representational medium — a way of constructing, expressing, and communicating ideas.

It is not a skill layered on top of a discipline.
It is inseparable from how the discipline thinks.

In disease ecology: you cannot fully understand an SIR model by reading its equations.
You have to build it, run it, break it, and interpret it to understand what it means.

Computation is reshaping scientific practice

In the last 30 years, nearly every scientific field has developed a computational counterpart — bioinformatics, computational statistics, computational ecology.

The question is no longer whether students will use computation. It is whether they will understand what they are doing when they do.

What is computational literacy?

Computational literacy is not the same as knowing how to code.

It has three inseparable dimensions (Odden, Lockwood & Caballero, 2019):

Dimension Question In disease ecology
Material Can you use the tools? Write and run an ODE model in R
Cognitive Do you think with the tools? Know why \(\beta\) represents transmission?
Social Can you communicate through the tools? Annotate code for a collaborator; visualize results for a public health audience

All three dimensions must develop together. Material literacy alone — “I can run the code” — is not computational literacy.

What computational literacy is NOT

Not software training
Knowing how to use R, Python, or Excel is a starting point, not an endpoint. Tool fluency without conceptual grounding produces students who can execute code they do not understand.

Not generic computational thinking
Frameworks that teach decomposition, abstraction, and algorithmic thinking as general skills, detached from any discipline. These are valuable foundations, but they are not sufficient for scientific practice.

Not AI literacy
AI literacy concerns evaluating, using, and understanding AI systems. It overlaps with computational literacy but is distinct. (Hackl, Müller & Sailer, 2026)

Not mathematical fluency
A student can solve the SIR system analytically and still have no understanding of what \(\beta\) means biologically, or whether their code faithfully represents it.

Computational literacy in a discipline means moving fluently between biological meaning, mathematical formulation, and computational implementation — in both directions.

Computational thinking practices in science

Weintrop et al. (2016) identified four categories of computational thinking for science and mathematics classrooms:

Data practices — collecting, creating, manipulating, analyzing, and visualizing data
In disease ecology: fitting an SIR model to flu surveillance data

Modeling and simulation practices — using, assessing, designing, and constructing computational models
In disease ecology: building an ODE system from biological assumptions, then testing whether the model’s behavior is biologically plausible

Computational problem solving practices — abstraction, decomposition, debugging, choosing tools
In disease ecology: decomposing transmission into contact rate and probability of infection; debugging a model that produces negative compartment sizes

Systems thinking practices — understanding relationships, thinking in levels, managing complexity
In disease ecology: understanding how individual-level contact rates produce population-level epidemic curves

Why domain-specific computational literacy matters

Generic computational thinking tells you how to think computationally.

Domain-specific computational literacy tells you what to think about and what it means.

Consider debugging. In computer science, debugging means finding why the code does not run.

In disease ecology, debugging also means asking:

  • Why does my epidemic never take off even when \(R_0\) > 1?
  • Why does the recovered population exceed the population size?
  • Does this parameter estimate make biological sense?

These questions require disciplinary knowledge. They cannot be answered by someone who only knows how to code or only sees equations.

Why we built this module

The SIR model is one of the most studied objects in all of applied mathematics. Resources exist.

The gap is not content. The gap is the on-ramp.

Most existing resources assume the reader already thinks like a modeler. They begin with equations and ask students to follow. They treat computation as implementation of mathematics that is already understood.

This module starts from the other direction:

  • What biological process does this compartment represent?
  • What assumption produces this term in the equation?
  • What happens to the system if that assumption is violated?

And it is designed for students with no prior coding or disease ecology background, which means it is designed for most students in most classrooms.

Productive struggle and epistemic agency

When a student changes \(\beta\) and watches the epidemic curve shift in real time, something happens that reading cannot produce.

Productive struggle/failure (Kapur, 2010) — Learning is deeper when students encounter difficulty before receiving instruction. A live model generates the questions that make explanation meaningful.

Epistemic agency (Odden & Malthe-Sørenssen, 2021) — Students are not consuming knowledge, they are generating it. Every time a student runs the model, adjusts a parameter, and interprets what changed, they are doing what scientists do.

This is why the interactive code blocks in this module are not optional enrichment. They are the pedagogical core.

Change one number. Hit Run. What happened? Why?

What we built

An open, browser-based disease ecology module — no installation, no prior background required.

Built using Quarto with WebR — live R code that runs in the browser.

Covers:

  1. Epidemiological vocabulary and compartmental models
  2. The SIR epidemic model and its biological assumptions
  3. Solving ODE systems numerically

This is the first module in a series — a foundation for SEIR models, spillover dynamics, parameter estimation, and spatial spread.

Currently in beta. Available at github.com/javirudolph/ecoSIR-modules. Coming to QUBES Hub.
